Why this needs to be Stopped

T.O. v. Fort Bend Independent School District

A 7-year-old boy, a special education student, was sent out of his class for being disruptive. A teacher stood in the hallway blocking the classroom door, and the little boy tried pushing past her to re-enter the class. This 200+ lb. teacher slammed him to the floor and choked him for over a minute, until bruises ringed his neck. Because this type of excessive force had never been inflicted on a child by a teacher before, the parents of the boy were unable to sue the teacher or the school district.

Baxter v. Bracey

Police pursued a man into the basement of a house. They found him on his knees, with his hands over his head in surrender. They released a police dog on him anyway and the man was torn apart. Although a previous case deemed such wanton brutality a 4th amendment violation, the new case was not similar enough: Baxter had been on his knees, whereas in the previous case, the suspect had been lying down before the dog was released on him. Thus, qualified immunity protected the officer and the police department from a lawsuit.

Hernandez v. Department of Protective and Regulatory Services

A young couple brought their injured baby to the hospital. Nurses were concenred that perhaps the child had been abused, so Child Protective Services placed the baby in the custody of a foster family. While in foster custody, the baby was left face down on a pillow to suffocate and die. Even though numerous complaints had been made about the foster family's negligent care prior to this tragedy, the CPS social worker, her supervisor, and CPS itself were all immune from suit.
